## 1. Overview

This category covers users who want to be **simultaneously strong and conditioned** — hybrid athletes, CrossFit-style trainees, tactical/military populations, recreational multi-sport athletes, and general-population people whose definition of "fit" includes both a respectable squat and a respectable 5K/row. The goal is a *wide ceiling* across multiple qualities, not a high ceiling in one.

**Primary adaptations and their tension:**
- **Strength / hypertrophy / power** (as in `general_strength.md`).
- **Aerobic** — VO2max, mitochondrial density, capillarization, stroke volume, economy.
- **Anaerobic** — lactate threshold, work capacity, repeatability.

### The Interference Effect — the central programming problem

Hickson (1980) first reported that concurrent strength + endurance training can blunt strength/power adaptations relative to strength training alone. Current evidence — important nuances for this category:

- **Hypertrophy is largely robust.** Schumann et al. (2022; 43 studies) — concurrent training did *not* meaningfully reduce hypertrophy or maximal strength across ages, fitness levels, or endurance modality. The interference effect "is getting less scary by the day" (Stronger By Science).
- **Power is the most interference-sensitive quality** — small-to-moderate impairment across most populations, including in trained strength athletes specifically for power.
- **Maximal strength** showed blunting in untrained populations and in endurance-trained athletes, but **did not reach significance in trained strength athletes** — experienced lifters are relatively protected.
- **Sex differences:** lower-body strength blunting appeared in males but not females; power impairment occurred in both.
- **Modality:** running tends to interfere slightly more than cycling (eccentric loading and inflammatory cost), though the gap is smaller than once believed. **Cycling/rowing are the safer choices when hypertrophy/strength is the priority.**
- **Magnitude driver:** total endurance *dose* and *proximity to failure/fatigue* matter most. Interference is dose-dependent.

**The one-sentence summary the Haiku agent should internalize:** for general-fitness and endurance goals, concurrent training is safe; for power-focused users, it is the **power** quality that needs protection, via session separation, sequencing, and dose control.

## 2. Programming by experience tier

### BEGINNER

| Variable | Prescription |
|---|---|
| Frequency | 3–4 total sessions/week. E.g., 2–3 strength + 1–2 conditioning, on separate days if possible. |
| Strength volume | 8–12 hard sets/muscle/week, full-body. Beginners interfere with themselves mostly by overdoing total load. |
| Strength intensity | 65–75% 1RM, 8–12 reps, RPE 6–8. |
| Conditioning | Build an aerobic base. **Mostly Zone 2** (65–75% max HR, "can talk not sing"). Start ~90–120 min/week, building toward the 150 min/week moderate-intensity health minimum. Minimal high-intensity work until base and lifting technique are established. |
| Split | Full-body strength 3× + standalone easy conditioning. Avoid heavy legs and hard intervals on the same day. |
| Periodization | Linear progression on lifts; progressive time/distance on conditioning. One quality at a time per session. |
| Sequencing | Easiest lever for beginners — **put strength and hard conditioning on different days entirely.** If combined, lift first, keep cardio easy and <30 min. |
| Deload | Reactive; an easy week when run-down. |
| Weekly time | **3.5–5 hrs/week.** |

### INTERMEDIATE

| Variable | Prescription |
|---|---|
| Frequency | 5–6 sessions/week. Common sweet spot: **4 lifting + 2–3 conditioning**, some doubling up on the same day. |
| Strength volume | 10–18 hard sets/muscle/week. Slightly below what a pure-strength intermediate would run, to leave recovery headroom. |
| Strength intensity | Full range — heavy 3–6 reps at 78–87%, plus 6–12 rep hypertrophy work; RPE 7–9. |
| Conditioning | **Polarized: ~80% easy (Zone 2), ~20% hard.** Target ~150–250 min/week total. Typically 1–2 Zone 2 sessions (30–60 min) + 1 hard interval/threshold/metcon session. Keep most volume easy — easy aerobic work has minimal interference; hard intervals carry the most. |
| Split structure | Upper/Lower lifting + conditioning slotted by the **High-Low model** — cluster high-CNS stressors (heavy lower-body + hard intervals) on the same or adjacent days, then follow with genuinely low/recovery days. "Better to be wrecked Monday and recovered Wednesday than kind of tired every day." |
| Periodization | Weekly undulating on strength; **the "training see-saw"** — when you push strength hard in a block, dial conditioning back to maintenance, and vice versa. Don't push both maximally at once. |
| Deload | Every 4–6 weeks; deload *both* qualities, or stagger (deload strength one week, conditioning the next). |
| Weekly time | **6–9 hrs/week.** |

#### Interference-management rules (intermediate — the critical section)
- **Separate hard strength and hard conditioning by ≥6 hours, ideally 24h**, when possible.
- **If same session: lift first, condition second** — preserves heavier loads; lifting first then HIIT has been shown to improve both strength and VO2max.
- **Prioritize power/explosive work at the very start of a session, fully fresh** — power is the most interference-sensitive quality.
- **Pair non-competing muscle groups** when stacking — e.g., a run (lower body) before an upper-body lift interferes less than run + leg day.
- **Prefer cycling/rowing over running** when a hard strength block is the priority, to limit eccentric-damage interference.
- **Cap hard conditioning dose** — interference is dose-dependent; keep same-day cardio short (<30 min) if it follows lifting.

### ADVANCED

| Variable | Prescription |
|---|---|
| Frequency | 6–10+ sessions/week, frequently **two-a-days** (this is how the ≥6h separation is actually achieved — e.g., AM lift, PM conditioning). |
| Strength volume | 12–20+ sets/muscle/week, cycled; managed against total systemic load including conditioning. |
| Strength intensity | Full spectrum, cycled in blocks; RPE-range autoregulation. |
| Conditioning | 250–400+ min/week, strongly polarized (~80/20). Multiple Zone 2 sessions + 2 quality sessions (intervals, threshold, sport-specific metcons). Modality chosen deliberately by which strength quality is being protected in the current block. |
| Split structure | Block-emphasis with two-a-days; High-Low strictly enforced; hard days truly hard, easy days truly easy. |
| Periodization | **Block periodization with concurrent emphasis shifts** — e.g., a strength-emphasis block (conditioning at maintenance: 2 short sessions/week) alternating with a conditioning-emphasis block (strength at maintenance: ~2 sessions, ~6 sets/muscle/week to *maintain*). Maintenance volume is low — this is what makes concurrent peaking feasible. |
| Interference management | All intermediate rules, plus: strict session separation via two-a-days; never schedule hard intervals within ~24h of a heavy lower-body session; protect power work absolutely (its own day, fully fresh, or front of session); autoregulate — concurrent fatigue is systemic and compounds fast. |
| Deload | Every 3–4 weeks, reactive-leaning. Concurrent fatigue feeds two qualities — advanced hybrid athletes hit systemic MRV faster than single-discipline athletes. |
| Weekly time | **8–14+ hrs/week.** |

## 3. Ideal exercises + equipment ladders

The strength base **mirrors `general_strength.md`** — squat, hip hinge/deadlift, horizontal & vertical press, horizontal & vertical pull, carries, single-leg. Use those ladders directly. The Mixed category *adds* power and conditioning movements:

**Olympic lift / explosive triple-extension (power)** — trains rate of force development; the most interference-sensitive quality, so it gets priority placement.
Power clean, push press, hang clean → barbell power clean / high pull → DB clean & press, DB snatch, KB swing/clean → broad jump, vertical jump, tuck jump.

**Kettlebell swing / ballistic hinge (power-endurance bridge)** — bridges strength and conditioning; explosive hinge that also drives heart rate.
KB swing, cable hip-extension throws → KB or DB swing → DB swing, banded hip-thrust speed reps → speed hip thrust, jump.

**Loaded carry (work capacity + grip + trunk)** — high transfer to hybrid/tactical demands; trains conditioning under load.
Farmer's carries, sandbag carries, sled push/drag → loaded barbell/DB carries → DB/KB carries → bear crawl, weighted-pack ruck walk.

**Cyclical conditioning ergometer (low-interference aerobic)** — builds the engine with minimal eccentric muscle damage; preferred when protecting strength.
Rower, assault/echo bike, ski erg, SkiErg → stationary bike, rower (if owned), jump rope → jump rope, shuttle runs → running, hill sprints, burpees.

**Running (sport-specific aerobic/anaerobic)** — highest real-world carryover for many; **note: highest-interference modality**, so program the dose deliberately.
Treadmill (incline control, intervals) → outdoor running → outdoor running → outdoor running (it's free).

**Conditioning circuit / metcon (anaerobic work capacity)** — trains repeatable effort and the ability to "put fitness back together" under fatigue.
Barbell/DB complexes, machine circuits, rower intervals → barbell complexes, EMOMs → DB complexes, band circuits → burpees, air squats, push-ups, mountain climbers in AMRAP/EMOM formats.

## 4. Common mistakes by tier

**Beginner:** doing strength and hard conditioning in the same session every day fresh off the couch — guaranteeing both fatigue and interference; all conditioning done "hard" — no aerobic base, everything a sprint; treating CrossFit-style metcons as the *entirety* of training and never running actual strength progressions; too much total volume too soon; no easy days.

**Intermediate:** trying to PR the lift *and* go 100% on the metcon multiple times a week — a fast track to burnout — instead of using the see-saw; ignoring the High-Low principle and stacking moderate-hard work every day so they're chronically "kind of tired" and never fully fresh; scheduling hard intervals the day before or same day as heavy squats/deadlifts; doing power/explosive work *after* conditioning or deep in a fatigued session — directly sabotaging the most interference-sensitive quality; letting conditioning volume creep so high it interferes; running everything when cycling/rowing would protect strength; never separating sessions even when schedule allows the ≥6h gap.

**Advanced:** trying to peak both qualities simultaneously instead of using block-emphasis shifts with maintenance phases; two-a-days with insufficient inter-session recovery or nutrition; not respecting that concurrent MRV arrives faster than single-discipline MRV — deloading too late; modality dogma — refusing to swap running for lower-impact ergometers during heavy strength blocks; failing to drop strength or conditioning to true *maintenance* (~6 sets/muscle, ~2 short cardio sessions) during the other quality's emphasis block — "maintaining" at near-peak volume and recovering from nothing.
