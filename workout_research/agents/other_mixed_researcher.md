## Standing brief (pass this verbatim)

Research the evidence base for concurrent strength + endurance training. Open with the overview and the **interference effect** literature — clearly distinguish current evidence: hypertrophy and maximal strength are largely robust (for trained lifters); **power is the casualty**; modality (cycling/rowing vs running) and dose matter. Deliver for each tier: frequency (total sessions/week), strength volume + intensity, conditioning volume + intensity distribution (Zone 2 dominant for beginners; ~80/20 polarized at intermediate+), split structure, periodization including **the see-saw model** (push one quality, maintain the other), the **High-Low model**, deload guidance, realistic weekly time. **Spell out the interference-management rules** as a numbered section: ≥6h separation (ideally 24h or different days); lift-first when concurrent in one session; protect power work first/fresh; pair non-competing muscle groups; prefer cycling/rowing over running during strength blocks; cap hard cardio dose. Add the Mixed-specific exercise additions (power, KB swing, loaded carry, ergometer, running, metcon) with equipment ladders. Conclude with common mistakes per tier and sources.

## Settled points

- The interference effect is **real but modest and manageable**; hypertrophy and trained-lifter strength are largely protected. Power is the fragile quality.
- Three management levers: **separation, sequencing, dose.**
- High-Low and see-saw are the organizing scheduling principles.
- Maintenance volume is low (~6 sets/muscle/week, ~2 short cardio sessions) — this is what makes concurrent peaking feasible.
- Advanced concurrent athletes hit systemic MRV faster than single-discipline athletes — deload accordingly.

## Known-issue watchlist

- The interference literature has shifted notably in the last 5 years; expect more updates. Re-check every refresh whether power-specific impairment magnitude estimates have moved.
- Hybrid-athlete coaching is a fast-evolving niche; influencer-led claims are common. Keep the source bar high.
- Watch for new evidence on protein/nutrient timing as an interference modifier.
