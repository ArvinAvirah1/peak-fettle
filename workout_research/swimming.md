## 1. Sport overview

Swimming is unique among endurance disciplines because **technique/efficiency dominates outcomes far more than in running or cycling**. The reason is physics:

**The governing equation.** Maximum swimming velocity = (energy input) × (propelling efficiency ÷ hydrodynamic drag). The human body is a terrible propeller in water — propelling efficiency is very low — so small technical improvements yield outsized speed gains. Water is ~800× denser than air, so drag is the primary enemy. A moderately fit swimmer with great technique routinely beats a much fitter swimmer with poor technique.

**Evidence on the technique-vs-fitness split.** In youth swimmers, biomechanical factors explained **~90% of variance in 100m performance**, versus ~46% for anthropometric and ~45% for physiological factors. A swim program that doesn't allocate significant time to technique is mis-built — this is the defining feature distinguishing swim programming from land-sport programming.

**The three determinant categories:**

1. **Technical / biomechanical (the biggest lever).** Body position / streamline (sinking hips/legs is the single most costly fault); stroke efficiency (distance-per-stroke, stroke index); the **high-elbow catch / early vertical forearm** (signature of world-class freestyle/fly); body rotation along the long axis; breathing mechanics that don't disrupt alignment.
2. **Physiological.** Aerobic capacity (VO2peak); anaerobic capacity / lactate tolerance; propulsive force, especially leg-kick force and pulling force.
3. **Anthropometric.** Height, wingspan, hand/foot size, buoyancy — largely fixed and not trainable, but they explain why technique is the *trainable* lever.

**For app users the trainable levers, in priority order:** (1) technical efficiency / drag reduction, (2) aerobic base, (3) threshold/lactate capacity, (4) sprint power and kick force, (5) dryland strength.

**Intensity distribution:** roughly **80/20** (≈80% easy/aerobic, ≈20% threshold-and-above) for general fitness and distance focus. Technique work is threaded through *every* session at every tier.

## 2. CSS — the practical threshold anchor

**Critical Swim Speed (CSS)** is the pace a swimmer could hold for a 1500m time trial; it serves the role FTP plays in cycling. Zones:

- Recovery: **65–75% CSS**
- Endurance: **75–90% CSS**
- Threshold: **90–100% CSS**

Example: CSS 1:20/100m → threshold 1:20–1:28/100m, recovery 1:47–2:00/100m.

## 3. Programming by experience tier

### BEGINNER (cannot yet swim 1,500m continuously; or new to structured swim training)

| Variable | Prescription |
|---|---|
| Goal | Build water comfort, efficient breathing, basic freestyle technique, weekly routine. Milestone: swim 1,500m continuously — most reach this in **8–16 weeks of consistent 3×/week training**. |
| Frequency | 2–3 sessions/week. |
| Volume | ~3,000–6,000m/week total (often broken, not continuous). Individual sessions 1,000–2,000m. |
| Intensity | Very technique-heavy. ~70–80% easy aerobic + drills; ~20% light "build" efforts. Almost no true high-intensity work yet — technique breaks down under fatigue and reinforces bad patterns. |
| Key sets | Drill sets dominate; short aerobic swims with generous rest (10–20 × 25–50m, 15–30s rest); kick sets. |
| Periodization | A long, simple base phase. High volume relative to ability, low intensity. |
| Progression | Add distance / reduce rest before adding intensity. Extend continuous-swim distance week over week. |
| Deload | Every 4th week, cut volume ~30–40%. |
| Weekly time | 1.5–3 hours. |

### INTERMEDIATE (can comfortably swim 1,500m continuously)

| Variable | Prescription |
|---|---|
| Goal | Build aerobic engine; introduce structured threshold work; refine technique under moderate fatigue. |
| Frequency | 3–4 sessions/week. |
| Volume | ~6,000–12,000m/week. Sessions 1,800–3,000m. |
| Intensity | ~80/20. Threshold/CSS work becomes a regular weekly fixture (1–2×/week). |
| Key sets | Aerobic base sets (build 50s/100s); **threshold/CSS sets** (4–10 × 100m at CSS, 15–20s rest); drill/technique sets every session; introductory VO2/sprint work in small doses; kick sets. |
| Periodization | Distinct base phase (high volume, low intensity) → build phase (6–8 weeks: volume holds, intensity rises, threshold/lactate sets regular). |
| Progression | Classic CSS build — e.g., 10 × 100m CSS reached by adding one 100m per week over 10 weeks. |
| Deload | Every 3rd–4th week, ~30% volume cut. |
| Weekly time | 3–5 hours. |

### ADVANCED (competitive or highly trained)

| Variable | Prescription |
|---|---|
| Goal | Event-specific peaking; simultaneous development of 2–3 priority qualities per block. |
| Frequency | 4–6+ sessions/week (4–5 is the realistic ceiling for a fitness-app "advanced" user). |
| Volume | 12,000m+ (recreational-advanced). Often 4,000m+ across 4–5 sessions as a floor. |
| Intensity | Event-dependent — **sprinters: polarized + threshold**; **middle-distance: threshold + pyramidal**; **distance: pyramidal**. Full use of all zones including VO2max and race-pace/sprint. |
| Key sets | All of the above plus dedicated **VO2max sets** (10 × 50m at 90–95%, 30s rest; 3 × 300m hard, 4 min rest) and **race-pace / sprint sets**; broken swims (e.g., 100 + 2×50 to train a 200). |
| Periodization | **Block periodization** — season split into 2–4 macrocycles of 8–15 weeks, each divided into 2–5 week mesocycles, each developing 2–3 priority qualities, interspersed with short reduced-load periods. General → specific progression. |
| Progression | Manipulate volume/intensity/density wave-like across mesocycles; sharpen toward race pace late. |
| Deload / taper | Short reduced-load periods between mesocycles; **2-week taper before championship** competition (volume drops sharply, intensity/sharpness maintained). |
| Weekly time | 5–10+ hours. |

## 4. Key sessions, drills, and the equipment ladder

### Highest-value pool sets

| Set type | Example prescription | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Technique / drill | 8–12 × 25–50m single-focus drills, full rest | Efficiency, drag reduction — *the* swim priority |
| Aerobic base | Build 50s/100s, 15–30s rest, start easy → fast finish; or 20–30 min continuous | Aerobic engine; doubles as a threshold-pace test |
| Threshold / CSS | 4–10 × 100m at CSS, 15–20s rest; 8 × 50m at CSS, 10–15s rest; 3 × 500m | Aerobic capacity without deep fatigue; 1–2×/week |
| VO2max | 10 × 50m at 90–95%, 30s rest; 3 × 300m hard, 4 min rest | Max oxygen uptake; advanced only |
| Sprint / race-pace | Short max efforts; broken swims (100 + 2×50) | Speed, lactate tolerance, race specificity |
| Kick sets | Kickboard or streamline kick repeats | Leg-kick force, body position |

### Highest-value drills (freestyle focus)
- **6-kick / 2-stroke** (with fins) — one arm front, one at side, 6 kicks, then 2 strokes; teaches body position and rotation timing.
- **Over-the-wave / under-the-wave** — deliberately lift head on the breath, then bury head after; teaches head control and breathing rhythm.
- **Catch-up, single-arm, fingertip-drag, sculling** — standard catch and body-position drills.
- **Kick on side / streamline kick** — body position and rotation.

### Equipment ladder

**Tier 1 — Full pool + toys (pull buoy, paddles, fins, kickboard).** Pull buoy isolates the pull and reinforces body position; paddles increase propulsive load and reinforce the catch (use conservatively — shoulder risk); fins allow drill work at speed and build kick and ankle flexibility; kickboard isolates and develops the kick.

**Tier 2 — Basic pool, no toys.** All drills can be done toy-free (sculling, single-arm, catch-up, kick-on-side, streamline kick without a board). Use tempo and stroke-count constraints instead of equipment ("hold 16 strokes per length"). Vertical kicking develops the kick without a board.

**Tier 3 — Open water.** Continuous aerobic and threshold swims; sighting practice; pace by feel or stroke rate (no walls/clock). Best for aerobic base and threshold; hard to do precise intervals or drills.

**Tier 4 — NO pool access (dryland swim-fitness maintenance).** Critical fallback. You cannot fully replicate swimming on land, but you can maintain a large fraction of swim-specific fitness:
- **Swim-specific ergometer (Vasa trainer / swim bench)** — closest land substitute; trains the actual pull pattern and aerobic system in a swim-specific way. Best single option if available.
- **Stretch cords / resistance bands (swim-pull pattern)** — mimic the freestyle/fly pull; the most accessible swim-specific land tool. Use interval-style pulling sets to maintain the pattern and pulling endurance.
- **General cardio to hold the aerobic engine** — running, cycling, **rowing (best transfer — similar posterior-chain pull + aerobic demand)**, elliptical. Keep aerobic and threshold structure (80/20).
- **Mobility work** — shoulder and ankle/thoracic mobility, so technique isn't lost when returning to water.
- Expect some loss of "feel for water" on return — aerobic capacity, pulling strength, and pattern can be largely preserved for weeks; sport-specific efficiency cannot.

### Dryland strength support (all tiers benefit — adds ~2–2.5% over swim-only)
Recommended **2–3 sessions/week**. Equipment ladder:

| Category | Full equipment | Minimal / bodyweight |
|---|---|---|
| Upper-body pull (mirrors freestyle/fly pull) | Lat pulldown, cable rows, weighted pull-ups | Pull-ups, inverted rows, band rows |
| Upper-body push | Bench press, weighted dips | Push-ups, bench dips |
| Posterior chain (starts, streamline) | Deadlifts, hip thrusts | Single-leg RDLs, glute bridges, hip hinges |
| Core / anti-rotation (postural control, hydrodynamics) | Cable rotations, weighted carries, med-ball rotational throws | Planks, side planks, dead bugs, hollow holds |
| Power / plyometrics (speed, starts) | Box jumps, med-ball throws | Jump squats, broad jumps, clap push-ups |

Core gets disproportionate emphasis in swimming — it's what holds the streamlined, stable, rotating body position that reduces drag.

## 5. Common mistakes by tier

**Beginner:** **Looking forward / head too high** → hips and legs sink → massive drag (the #1 fault); lifting or twisting the head to breathe → shoulders and hips swing out of line; taking too long to inhale → head lifts, lead arm stalls, momentum dies; not kicking or kicking from the knees → legs drop; no body rotation — swimming "flat"; training intensity before competence — fatigue cements bad patterns.

**Intermediate:** neglecting technique once "fit enough" — efficiency plateaus while fitness rises; too much "gray-zone" middle-intensity swimming; over-reliance on paddles/fins masking technique faults; inconsistent CSS/threshold pacing — going out too hard, fading; skipping dryland and ignoring shoulder health.

**Advanced:** junk volume — accumulating yardage without intensity purpose; poor periodization — no distinct blocks, no taper, training "hard all the time"; inadequate recovery between high-intensity sets/sessions; letting technique degrade under race-pace fatigue without correcting it; mis-matching intensity distribution to event (a sprinter training like a distance swimmer).
