## 1. Sport overview

Distance-running performance is governed by three primary physiological variables:

- **VO2max** — the ceiling of aerobic capacity. The best single discriminator between low- and high-level runners, but it loses predictive power among athletes of similar ability. Improved most by VO2max intervals and high aerobic volume.
- **Lactate threshold (LT) / fractional utilization** — the percentage of VO2max sustainable before lactate accumulates faster than it clears. Determines sustainable race pace; for 10K–marathon it is arguably the most performance-relevant lever. Improved by threshold work and aerobic volume.
- **Running economy (RE)** — the oxygen cost of running at a given pace. Can vary up to ~30% among runners with the same VO2max, and accounts for up to ~65% of performance variation in similar-VO2max groups. Improved by high mileage, strength training, plyometrics, and technique.

Secondary determinants: maximal aerobic speed, neuromuscular power, durability (economy late in a long effort), anaerobic capacity (more relevant for 800m–5K).

**The 80/20 foundation:** elite-athlete research (Seiler) converges on ~80% of training volume at low intensity and ~20% at moderate-to-high intensity. The single biggest amateur error is running easy days too hard and hard days too easy — the "moderate-intensity rut."

## 2. Zone model (Jack Daniels' 5-zone system — the running standard)

- **Easy (E)** — recovery runs, warm-ups/cool-downs, long runs. ~59–74% VO2max / ~65–79% HRmax. Conversational. The bulk of all running.
- **Marathon (M)** — goal marathon pace; used in marathon-specific long-run segments.
- **Threshold (T)** — "comfortably hard," sustainable ~60 min in a race. ~83–88% VO2max / ~88–92% HRmax. Done as tempo runs (20–40 min continuous) or cruise intervals (5–6 × 1 mile w/ 1 min jog).
- **Interval (I)** — VO2max work, 3–5 min reps at ~95–100% VO2max (~3K–5K race pace), equal-duration recovery jog. E.g., 5 × 1000m or 6 × 800m.
- **Repetition (R)** — faster than I; anaerobic/speed and economy work, short reps (200–400m) with full recovery. E.g., 8 × 200m.

A simplified 3-zone polarized framing also works: Zone 1 (easy, below LT1), Zone 2 ("no man's land," avoid), Zone 3 (hard, above LT2).

## 3. Programming by experience tier

### BEGINNER (0–~12 months, or building from non-runner)

| Variable | Prescription |
|---|---|
| Frequency | 3 runs/week, non-consecutive days. Run/walk intervals are legitimate and evidence-supported (Couch-to-5K model). |
| Volume | Start ~5–15 miles/week, or 60–120 min/week total. |
| Intensity | Essentially all easy. The "hard" element early on is simply running continuously. No structured speedwork until a continuous 30-min run is comfortable. |
| Key sessions | Two easy run/walk or easy continuous runs + one slightly longer easy effort. No tempo or intervals yet. |
| Periodization | A single linear build of run duration; no formal base/build/peak needed for a first 5K. |
| Progression | The **10% rule** — do not increase weekly mileage more than ~10% per week (>10% jumps significantly raise injury rates). Hold volume steady some weeks. Add 1–3 cross-training days for aerobic stimulus without impact. |
| Deload | Every 3–4 weeks, hold or slightly reduce volume. |
| Weekly time | ~1.5–3 hours including cross-training. |

### INTERMEDIATE (consistent ~1–3 years, racing 5K–half marathon)

| Variable | Prescription |
|---|---|
| Frequency | 4–5 runs/week. |
| Volume | ~20–40 miles/week (~3.5–6 hrs). |
| Intensity | ~80/20 polarized or pyramidal. ~80% easy; ~20% split between threshold (the highest-value quality work for this tier) and some VO2max work. |
| Key sessions/wk | 1 long run (20–30% of weekly volume, easy, optionally finishing with an M-pace segment); 1 threshold session (20–30 min continuous tempo, or 4–5 × 1 mile cruise intervals); 1 VO2max session every 1–2 weeks (e.g., 5 × 1000m at I pace, equal jog recovery); remaining runs easy/recovery. |
| Periodization | 16-week macrocycle is standard for a goal race — Base (aerobic volume + strides, 6–8 wks) → Build (add threshold then intervals, 4–6 wks) → Peak (highest volume, race-pace specificity, ~2 wks) → Taper (2–3 weeks, cut volume ~40–60% while keeping intensity). |
| Progression | 10% rule still applies; alternate hard/easy days; never two quality sessions back-to-back. |
| Deload | Every 3–4 weeks reduce volume ~20–30% ("3:1" loading pattern). |
| Weekly time | ~4–7 hours/week. |

### ADVANCED (3+ years, competitive)

| Variable | Prescription |
|---|---|
| Frequency | 6–7 runs/week, often with doubles. |
| Volume | ~40–70+ miles/week (recreational-competitive); elites go far higher. |
| Intensity | Still ~80/20. Many lean **pyramidal** (more threshold volume) during base/build, shifting toward polarized as racing nears. |
| Key sessions | 2–3 quality sessions/week — threshold (longer tempos, 6+ mile cruise-interval sets), VO2max intervals, race-pace-specific work (M-pace long-run segments for marathoners, R-pace for 5K specialists), plus strides/hill sprints. Marathoners often use reverse periodization, keeping aerobic volume central. |
| Periodization | Full multi-mesocycle plan; double-periodized seasons (two race peaks). |
| Progression | Monitor acute:chronic load, resting HR, sleep, mood — advanced runners are *more* prone to overtraining due to discipline/motivation. |
| Deload | Every 3–4 weeks; longer down-week after each race block. |
| Weekly time | ~7–12+ hours/week. |

## 4. Key sessions, equipment alternatives, and strength support

### Highest-value workout types
1. **Easy/recovery run** — 30–90 min conversational. Builds aerobic base, capillaries, economy. The majority of every plan at every tier.
2. **Long run** — 60–150 min easy (marathoners up to ~2.5–3 hr); 20–30% of weekly volume. Builds durability, fat oxidation, glycogen storage.
3. **Tempo / threshold run** — 20–40 min continuous at T pace, or cruise intervals (5–6 × 1 mile w/ 1 min jog). Raises lactate threshold — the bread-and-butter quality session.
4. **VO2max intervals** — 3–5 min reps at I pace (5 × 1000m, 6 × 800m) with equal-duration jog recovery. Lifts the aerobic ceiling.
5. **Repetitions / strides / hill sprints** — 8–10 × 100–200m fast w/ full recovery, or 8–12 × 10–15 sec uphill sprints. Improves economy, neuromuscular power, leg stiffness; low aerobic cost.
6. **Progression run** — start easy, finish at M or T pace. Teaches pacing and fatigue resistance.

### Equipment ladder
Running needs only shoes. **Treadmill** is a near-perfect substitute (set 1–2% incline to offset the lack of air resistance); good for precise pace control and bad weather. Indoor track works for intervals. **No ability to run** (injury, no space): maintain aerobic fitness with cross-training — stationary bike, elliptical, **pool running** (deep-water running is the closest non-impact analog and is used by injured elites), rowing, brisk incline walking, or bodyweight HIIT / jump rope (10 min rope-skipping ≈ 30 min jogging in some research).

### Strength-training support (evidence-based — improves running economy ~2–6%)
Meta-analyses show heavy resistance training and plyometrics each improve running economy ~2–6% and improve time-trial performance and durability. Protocol: **2–3 sessions/week, 8–14 weeks**, low-rep heavy strength + plyometrics, programmed away from key runs so it adds little aerobic fatigue. Heavy near-maximal loads (≥90% 1RM) tend to beat lighter loads; plyometrics especially help at lower speeds.

Equipment ladder for the strength work: **Full gym** — back squat, deadlift/RDL, split squat, calf raise (3–5 sets of 3–6 reps heavy), plus box jumps, depth jumps, hops. **Dumbbells/kettlebell** — goblet squat, single-leg RDL, walking lunges, step-ups, heavy calf raises. **Bodyweight** — single-leg/pistol squats (or assisted), Bulgarian split squats, single-leg calf raises, single-leg hip thrusts, plus plyometrics (squat jumps, bounding, pogo hops, skipping — nearly equipment-free and high-value). Core throughout: planks, side planks, dead bugs, bird dogs.

## 5. Common mistakes by tier

**Beginner:** running easy days too fast (no true easy zone); increasing volume too fast (violating the 10% rule); skipping rest days; no strength work; poor/old footwear; doing too much too soon → shin splints, runner's knee, stress reactions.

**Intermediate:** the "moderate rut" — every run at the same medium-hard pace, so easy isn't easy and hard isn't hard; two quality sessions too close together; neglecting the long run or running it too fast; ignoring strength/plyometrics; inconsistent week-to-week volume.

**Advanced:** chronic under-recovery and overtraining (ironically more vulnerable due to discipline); insufficient taper; too much VO2max/anaerobic work for marathon goals; racing too often without down-weeks; chasing mileage at the expense of quality and sleep.
