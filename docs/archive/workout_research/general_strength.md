## 1. Overview

General strength is general-population strength training — "get strong, look athletic, stay healthy" — *not* a competition total. **This is the correct default for any user who picks no specific competitive goal.**

**What it optimizes:** broad, durable strength across all major movement patterns; lean muscle mass; joint and connective-tissue health; metabolic health (insulin sensitivity, body composition, resting metabolic rate); and functional capacity for daily life and recreational sport. It prioritizes balanced development, sustainability, and real-world transfer over the maximal expression of a few lifts.

**Who it suits:** the large majority of gym-goers — people who want to feel strong, look athletic, and protect against age-related decline (sarcopenia, osteopenia).

**Primary adaptations:** neuromuscular efficiency (dominates the first 6–8 weeks); muscle-fiber hypertrophy (Type I and II, accumulating over months/years); increased bone mineral density and tendon/ligament stiffness; improved metabolic health.

**Headline finding (2026 ACSM Resistance Training Guidelines — first major update in 17 years):** the largest gains come simply from going *from nothing to something*. Training all major muscle groups **at least twice per week** matters far more than chasing a "perfect" program. This should anchor how the Haiku agent talks to beginners.

## 2. Programming by experience tier

> Tier is set by recovery capacity. **Beginner** while session-to-session linear progress holds (~0–6–12 months). **Intermediate** once that stalls and progress is weekly (~1–3 years). **Advanced** once near the genetic ceiling with slow, monthly-visible adaptation (3+ years).

### BEGINNER

| Variable | Prescription |
|---|---|
| Frequency | 2–3 full-body days/week. 3× is the sweet spot. |
| Volume | ~8–12 hard sets per muscle group per week. Even 1–3 sets per exercise drives robust early gains. |
| Intensity | 60–70% 1RM, 8–12 reps, RPE 6–8 (leave 2–4 reps in reserve — technique is still being learned). 2–3 min rest on compounds. |
| Split | Full body, every session — maximizes frequency-per-muscle and practice reps on the main lifts. |
| Periodization | **Linear progression** — add a small load each session or weekly on the main lifts as long as reps and form hold. |
| Deload | Not needed as a scheduled event; take a lighter week reactively if run-down or after illness/travel. |
| Weekly time | 3 sessions × 45–60 min ≈ **2.25–3 hrs/week**. |

### INTERMEDIATE

| Variable | Prescription |
|---|---|
| Frequency | 3–4 days/week. |
| Volume | **10–20 hard sets per muscle group per week** (the evidence-based intermediate band; dose-response is roughly linear within it). |
| Intensity | Mixed: strength-biased work at 75–87% 1RM, 3–6 reps; hypertrophy work at 67–80% 1RM, 6–12 reps; RPE 7–9. |
| Split | **Upper/Lower** (4 days) or full-body 3×. Each muscle trained ~2×/week. |
| Periodization | **Double progression** (add reps within a range, then add load and reset) and/or **weekly undulating periodization** — a heavy day, a moderate day, a higher-rep day. Progression is weekly, not session-to-session. |
| Deload | Scheduled and proactive every 4–6 weeks. Reduce volume ~40–60% and/or intensity; keep movement patterns. |
| Weekly time | 3–4 sessions × 60–75 min ≈ **3–5 hrs/week**. |

### ADVANCED

| Variable | Prescription |
|---|---|
| Frequency | 4–6 days/week. |
| Volume | Often 15–25+ sets per muscle/week, highly individual and approaching MRV quickly. MEV itself has risen — an advanced trainee may see no growth below ~12 sets/muscle/week. Volume must be cycled, not held flat. |
| Intensity | Full spectrum, cycled deliberately — 80–100% 1RM blocks for strength, down to 65–75% for hypertrophy accumulation. RPE-range autoregulation rather than fixed targets. |
| Split | Upper/Lower, Push/Pull/Legs, or specialized body-part splits; each muscle still ideally hit ≥2×/week. |
| Periodization | **Block periodization** or daily/weekly undulating periodization with systematic variation of volume and intensity over multi-week mesocycles. Progress may only be visible monthly. |
| Deload | Every 3–4 weeks (advanced lifters reach systemic MRV fast), with reactive deloading layered on top — respond to stalled lifts, achiness, poor sleep, dropped motivation. Volume reductions of 25% to >50%. |
| Weekly time | 4–6 sessions × 60–90 min ≈ **5–8+ hrs/week**. |

## 3. Ideal exercises + equipment substitution ladders

The general-strength program is built from one exercise per fundamental movement pattern. Each ladder runs **full commercial gym → home gym (barbell + rack) → minimal (DBs / bands) → bodyweight.**

**Squat (knee-dominant lower)** — highest-value lower-body builder; loads quads/glutes and trains whole-body bracing.
Barbell back squat → barbell back squat (rack) → goblet squat, DB front squat, banded squat → Bulgarian split squat, pistol-squat progressions, tempo air squats.

**Hip hinge / deadlift (hip-dominant lower)** — trains the entire posterior chain; best total-body strength expression.
Barbell deadlift / trap-bar deadlift → barbell conventional or RDL → DB Romanian deadlift, single-leg DB RDL, banded good morning → single-leg hip thrust, Nordic hamstring curl, back extension.

**Horizontal press (bench)** — primary upper-body push; chest, front delt, triceps.
Barbell bench / machine chest press → barbell bench (rack + bench) → DB bench press, DB floor press, banded push-up → push-up and progressions (deficit, archer, decline).

**Vertical press (overhead)** — shoulder strength and overhead stability; balances pressing.
Barbell overhead press / machine shoulder press → barbell OHP (rack) → DB shoulder press, seated DB press, banded OHP → pike push-up → handstand push-up progression.

**Vertical pull (pull-up / pulldown)** — lats and upper-back width; counterbalances pressing volume.
Pull-up / lat pulldown → pull-up bar on rack → band-assisted pull-up, banded pulldown → inverted row to pull-up progression, towel/door rows.

**Horizontal pull (row)** — mid-back thickness, postural health, shoulder integrity.
Barbell row / chest-supported machine row / cable row → barbell bent-over row, Pendlay row → DB single-arm row, banded row → inverted row (under a table/bar), feet-elevated for difficulty.

**Loaded carry / core anti-movement** — bracing, grip, trunk stability; high real-world transfer.
Farmer's carry handles, cable Pallof press → loaded barbell/DB carries, plank variations → DB/KB carries, banded Pallof press → plank, side plank, hollow hold, dead bug.

**Single-leg work (lunge / split squat)** — corrects asymmetries, builds knee/hip resilience, low spinal load.
Walking lunge, DB Bulgarian split squat, single-leg leg press → barbell split squat → DB reverse lunge, step-up → bodyweight split squat, step-up, shrimp-squat progression.

*Optional accessories that round out the program:* hip thrust (glutes), face pull / band pull-apart (shoulder health), biceps curl & triceps extension (arm development), calf raise.

## 4. Common mistakes by tier

**Beginner:** program-hopping before any program has had time to work; chasing soreness or "the burn" instead of progressive overload; ego-loading until technique breaks; skipping lower-body or pulling work in favor of "mirror muscles"; doing advanced-level volume in week one.

**Intermediate:** still trying to add weight every session after LP has expired, then stalling and getting frustrated; adding volume indefinitely without ever deloading; never tracking workouts so overload is guesswork; junk volume — many low-effort sets far from failure; never varying intensity (everything at the same moderate weight).

**Advanced:** volume creep past MRV ("more is always better"); ignoring reactive deload signals because the calendar says "accumulation week"; insufficient exercise variation leading to overuse injury and stale stimulus; under-recovering as load outpaces sleep/nutrition/stress management; over-specializing and losing balanced development and joint health.
