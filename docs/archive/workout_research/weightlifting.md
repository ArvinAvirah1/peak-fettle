## 1. Sport overview

Olympic weightlifting is contested in two lifts — the **snatch** (ground to overhead in one motion) and the **clean & jerk** (ground to shoulders, then overhead) — and scored on the **sum of the best snatch and best clean & jerk**, adjusted for bodyweight (Sinclair / IWF formulas) and weight class.

**What it optimizes:** maximal **power output** — moving a near-maximal load through a large range of motion at high velocity — combined with the precise technical skill to receive and stabilize that load in a deep position.

**Primary adaptations targeted:** rate of force development / explosive strength; maximal strength in supporting positions (squat strength is the foundation); motor skill and technical precision (far more practice reps required than powerlifting — skill is the limiter for most lifters); extreme mobility (ankle, hip, thoracic, shoulder, wrist); and postural strength/stability under fast, awkward loading.

**Key performance determinants:** technical efficiency (bar path, timing of the second pull, speed under the bar); lower-body maximal strength (back/front squat) as the strength ceiling; explosive power / RFD; mobility sufficient to hit the receiving positions; positional stability overhead and in the front rack; and consistency under competition pressure (3 attempts per lift, no second chances).

> **Critical equipment note for the app:** the Olympic lifts essentially **require a barbell + bumper plates + space**. Off-equipment substitutions (jumps, DB/KB variations, mobility work) train the *attributes* — power, posterior chain, positions — but cannot replace skill practice in the classical lifts. This asymmetry is much sharper than in powerlifting. When a weightlifting user has no barbell, the honest framing is "maintenance + attribute training," not "an equivalent routine."

## 2. Programming by experience tier

> Tier is set by technical competence and recovery capacity. **Beginner** = no stable technique, no reliable 1RMs. **Intermediate** = stable 1RMs, tolerates more volume, needs technical-variation work. **Advanced** = needs significant volume and regular 90%+ exposure.

### BEGINNER

| Variable | Prescription |
|---|---|
| Frequency | **3 days/week.** Technique practice matters more than load. Squat or front squat most sessions; the classical lifts and basic positional drills practiced each session. |
| Volume | Low. Main lifts ~3–5 sets of 2–3 reps; squat ~3–5 sets. Total session reps modest — the goal is quality movement, not accumulation. |
| Intensity | Light to moderate, ~60–75% 1RM (or, since 1RMs aren't reliable yet, "a weight you can move with perfect technique"). Singles and heavy attempts are rare. |
| Rep scheme | Classical lifts in singles, doubles, triples; squats and presses in 3–5s; positional/segment drills in 2–3s. |
| Periodization | Simple **linear progression** — add small load as technique allows. Sample 3-day split: Day 1 Snatch / Snatch Pull / Front Squat; Day 2 Jerk / Push Press / Overhead Squat; Day 3 Clean & Jerk / Clean Pull / Back Squat. |
| Progression | Add small increments when a lift is executed with sound technique at the prescribed reps — never on a fixed schedule, never at the cost of form. |
| Deload | Rarely needed at low volume; take an easy week if run-down. |
| Weekly time | ~3–4.5 hours (3 sessions, 60–90 min). |

### INTERMEDIATE

| Variable | Prescription |
|---|---|
| Frequency | **4–5 days/week.** Snatch, clean, and jerk variations most sessions; cleans in some form 2–3×/wk minimum; squatting 3–4×/wk. |
| Volume | Moderate and periodized — more than a beginner but not so much it breaks them. Classical lifts and pulls accumulate meaningful weekly set counts; wider use of variations (hang, blocks, pause, segment) to target weaknesses. |
| Intensity | Classical lifts cycle 70–90%, with periodic singles into the high 80s–low 90s. Pulls 90–105%. Squats 75–90%. Power variations for RFD at 55–70%. |
| Rep scheme | Classical lifts mostly singles and doubles (occasional triples); pulls 1–3 reps; squats 2–5 reps; accessories 6–10. |
| Periodization | **Block periodization.** Typical 4-week mesocycle: build 3 weeks then back off the 4th; or build 2, back off, then peak on the 4th. 2–3 weeks of "burdening" followed by a recovery week. |
| Progression | Across mesocycles — increase volume or intensity block-to-block; rotate variations to address technical faults; re-test maxes periodically. |
| Deload | Back-off week every ~4 weeks within the cycle. Plus longer deloading periods twice a year after the two main meets — 3+ weeks, 2–3 sessions/week at ~half the usual session length, different exercise selection. |
| Weekly time | ~5–8 hours (4–5 sessions, 75–110 min). |

### ADVANCED

| Variable | Prescription |
|---|---|
| Frequency | **6 days/week** is typical, sometimes with double sessions. Some form of snatch, clean, jerk, pull, and squat touched almost daily. |
| Volume | High — large weekly totals of classical lifts, pulls, and squats, tightly periodized between high-volume accumulation and low-volume intensity/peak blocks. |
| Intensity | High — classical lifts regularly 90–95%, occasional singles 95%+; pulls 105–115%; squats 85–95%. Frequent 90%+ exposure is required to maintain and push maxes. |
| Rep scheme | Almost entirely singles and doubles on the classical lifts; pulls 1–3; squats 1–3 in peak phases, up to 5 in accumulation. |
| Periodization | **Block periodization** with concentrated emphasis — accumulation → transmutation/intensification → realization/peak — plus a competition taper. Multi-block (3- and 4-block) models are standard. |
| Progression | Long-term, across competition cycles; managed via block design and variation selection rather than session-to-session load increases. |
| Deload | Built-in back-off weeks every 3–4 weeks; a competition taper (volume cut sharply, intensity carefully dosed to shed fatigue while keeping speed); post-meet deloading periods of 3+ weeks twice yearly. Larger/older/more advanced athletes need longer, deeper tapers. |
| Weekly time | ~8–14+ hours (6 sessions, 90–150 min, sometimes doubled). |

## 3. Ideal exercises + equipment substitution ladders

Ladders run: **full commercial gym → home gym (barbell + bumpers) → minimal (DBs / KBs / bands) → bodyweight.** For the classical lifts, the off-barbell options train transferable power but are *not* the sport skill — see the equipment note in §1.

### Competition lifts

**Snatch** — whole-body explosive power and overhead receiving.
Barbell snatch, platform, bumpers → identical (barbell + bumpers + space; rack optional) → single-arm DB snatch, KB snatch (transferable power pattern, different skill) → broad jumps, vertical jumps, overhead squat with a dowel for positions/mobility.

**Clean & Jerk** — max-load pull plus overhead drive.
Barbell clean & jerk, platform, bumpers → identical → double-DB clean & push press, KB clean & jerk, DB power clean → jump squats, explosive step-ups, plyometric push-ups, broad jumps.

### High-value accessories

**Back Squat** — primary lower-body strength base; the strength ceiling for both lifts.
Barbell back squat → barbell back squat → double-DB squat, goblet squat, DB Bulgarian split squat → pistol squats, tempo/paused squats, Bulgarian split squats.

**Front Squat** — directly supports the clean recovery and front-rack position.
Barbell front squat → barbell front squat → double-DB front-rack squat, goblet squat → heel-elevated tempo squat, Bulgarian split squat.

**Overhead Squat** — builds and tests the snatch receiving position; mobility + stability.
Barbell overhead squat → barbell overhead squat → single/double-DB overhead squat, KB overhead squat → dowel overhead squat, deep squat mobility holds, wall-supported drills.

**Snatch Pull / Clean Pull** — overloads the pull, reinforces bar path and positions, builds posterior chain.
Barbell snatch/clean pulls (often above 100% of the lift) → same → heavy DB RDL, DB high pull, banded hinge → single-leg RDL, broad jumps, back extensions.

**Push Press / Power Jerk** — overhead power and lockout for the jerk.
Barbell push press / power jerk → same → double-DB push press, KB push press → pike push-ups progressing to explosive/handstand push-ups.

**Strict / Overhead Press** — overhead stability and lockout strength.
Barbell strict press → barbell strict press → standing DB shoulder press, band overhead press → pike push-ups → handstand push-up progressions.

**Romanian Deadlift / Deadlift** — posterior-chain strength feeding the pull.
Barbell RDL/deadlift → same → DB RDL, single-leg DB RDL, banded RDL → single-leg RDL, Nordic curls, back extensions, hip thrusts.

**Pull-Up / Row (upper-back work)** — bar control, overhead stability, postural strength.
Pull-ups, barbell rows, pulldowns → pull-up bar, barbell rows → single-arm DB row, band pulldown, inverted rows → pull-ups/chin-ups, inverted rows.

**Plyometrics / Jumps** (box jumps, broad jumps, depth jumps) — rate of force development; the most equipment-free way to train the sport's core quality.
Box jumps, depth jumps → box jumps, depth jumps → broad jumps, tuck jumps → identical (bodyweight by nature).

**Core / trunk** (loaded carries, hanging leg raise, back extension, planks) — trunk stability to transmit force and hold positions.
Loaded carries, hanging leg raise, GHD/back extension, ab wheel → ab wheel, hanging leg raise → weighted plank, suitcase carry, band Pallof press → planks, hollow holds, leg raises, Superman holds.

**Mobility work** (ankle, hip, thoracic, shoulder, wrist) — a true performance determinant here, not optional.
All settings: goblet-squat holds, dowel passthroughs, ankle/wrist drills, banded distractions — minimal-to-no equipment required.

## 4. Common mistakes by tier

**Beginner:** lifting too heavy too soon — the single biggest mistake, since bad technical habits are very hard to unlearn; making load rather than movement quality the session goal; early arm bend in the pull; "leaving the bar out front" and reaching the hips to it; over-focusing on pulling the bar high instead of moving fast and pulling *under* it; using advanced/high-volume programs they cannot recover from or execute; neglecting mobility so receiving positions are never attainable.

**Intermediate:** insufficient variation work — not using hang/block/pause/segment lifts to target the specific fault holding the total back; skipping back-off weeks; letting squat strength lag and cap the classical lifts; random program selection; maxing too frequently instead of building through submaximal volume.

**Advanced:** poor fatigue management — mistimed or absent tapers; inadequate post-meet deloading; chasing intensity without the underlying volume/strength base; year-round high specificity causing overuse injuries (wrists, knees, shoulders, low back); ignoring autoregulation in a sport that depends on speed.
