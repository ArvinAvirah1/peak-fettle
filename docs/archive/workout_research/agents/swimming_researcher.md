## Standing brief (pass this verbatim)

Research the evidence base for swimming. Open with the determinants — emphasize that **technique/biomechanics dominates** (~90% of 100m performance variance in youth swimmers) and explain why (water density, low human propelling efficiency, drag as primary enemy). Cover the three determinant categories (technical/biomechanical — body position, stroke efficiency, high-elbow catch, rotation, breathing; physiological — VO2peak, anaerobic capacity, propulsive force; anthropometric — largely fixed). Introduce Critical Swim Speed (CSS) as the practical threshold anchor with its zone percentages. Deliver for each tier: frequency, weekly volume in meters/yards, intensity distribution (~80/20), key set types (technique/drill, aerobic base, threshold/CSS, VO2max, sprint/race-pace, kick), periodization (long base → build → advanced block periodization with 2-week taper), deload cadence, realistic weekly time. Include the milestone target for beginners (continuous 1,500m). Then deliver key sets and drills with prescriptions, and a four-tier equipment ladder: full pool + toys → basic pool → open water → NO pool (dryland — Vasa, bands, rowing, mobility). Include the dryland strength-support protocol with bodyweight options. **Be honest that no pool = maintenance and attribute training, not equivalence.** Conclude with common mistakes per tier and sources.

## Settled points

- Technique-led programming is the defining feature of swim training. Allocate technique work in *every* session at *every* tier.
- CSS is the practical threshold anchor for app users.
- Beginner milestone: continuous 1,500m, typically 8–16 weeks at 3×/wk.
- The four-tier equipment ladder (full pool + toys → basic pool → open water → dryland) is honest about what can and can't be substituted.
- Dryland strength adds ~2–2.5% over swim-only; core gets disproportionate emphasis (postural control of the streamlined body).
- Rowing has the best dryland transfer for aerobic + posterior-chain pull.

## Known-issue watchlist

- "Feel for water" loss during pool-absence periods is real but poorly quantified — keep the language honest, not pseudo-precise.
- New biomechanics literature on the high-elbow catch and stroke timing is active; revisit drill recommendations per refresh.
- Pool-access patterns vary geographically — keep the no-pool fallback robust.
